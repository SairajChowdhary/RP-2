Application of MK-N220 Nanoparticles as a Hemostatic Agent

By: Sairaj Raman Kumar Chowdhary

Under the supervision of Dr. Shantanu Kumar Behera

Core Objectives

Guiding the synthesis and characterization of novel nanocomposites for advanced biomedical applications.

Synthesis

To synthesize MK-NC composite powder through the Polymer-Derived Ceramics (PDC) route.

Characterization

To perform structural and micro-structural characterization of the as-prepared composite powder.

Nanoparticle Production

To produce MK-C(N220) nanoparticles specifically for bio-molecule applications.

Coagulation

To achieve superior blood clotting time and enhanced coagulation efficiency.

The Synthesis Pathway

The creation of MK-NC powder follows a precise, multi-stage process involving pre-ceramic polymers, nano-carbon, and controlled thermal treatments to achieve the desired nanocomposite structure.

Step 1: Mix MK Powder (PDC) with Ethanol to form a white gluey mass.

Step 2: Separately, mix N220 Nano-Carbon with Ethanol to create a black thixotropic mass.

Step 3: Combine both mixtures to form a black gluey mass (MK+NC).

Step 4: Add Tri-Ethyl Amine (TEA) as a cross-linking agent.

Step 5: Cross-link at 250°C in air for 2 hours.

Step 6: Grind the resulting powder to a fine consistency.

Step 7: Heat the powder at 800°C and 1000°C in air to obtain the final black MK-NC powder.

Structural & Microstructural Analysis

Investigating the material's properties through advanced characterization techniques.

XRD Analysis

X-ray diffraction analysis reveals the phase composition of the synthesized materials. For samples NMK8, NMK10, and NMK11, a broad reflection peak around 24.7° indicates the presence of amorphous carbon. In the NMK12 sample, heat-treated at 1200°C, sharp peaks emerge, indicating crystallization. A prominent SiO₂ (Cristobalite) peak appears at ~22°, along with peaks for SiC, demonstrating phase transformation at higher temperatures.

Microstructure & Raman Spectroscopy

FESEM Analysis: Microstructure analysis via FESEM shows that samples treated up to 1100°C (NMK11) consist of fine, aggregated nanoparticles. The NMK12 sample, however, displays a more crystalline, fragmented morphology due to the higher temperature treatment.

Raman Spectroscopy: The analysis confirmed the presence of distinct carbon phases. The D band (~1355 cm⁻¹) corresponds to disordered carbon, while the G band (~1588 cm⁻¹) represents graphitic carbon, providing insight into the carbon structure within the nanocomposite.

Hemostatic Application: Blood Clotting

Evaluating the nanoparticles' effectiveness in accelerating the natural coagulation cascade.

Clotting Mechanism

The procedure involves recalcification of blood samples to initiate coagulation. The nanoparticles are then introduced, and the time to form a stable clot is measured. This tests the material's ability to interact with platelets and clotting factors to form a fibrin mesh, effectively stopping blood flow.

Blood Clotting Time Results (in seconds)

Blood (Control) NMK8 NMK10 NMK11 NMK12

The results clearly demonstrate a significant reduction in clotting time with the application of the nanoparticles. The NMK11 and NMK12 samples, treated at higher temperatures, show the most potent hemostatic effect, reducing clotting time by over 70% compared to the control.

Future Directions

Advancing the research through further analysis and testing to validate the material for clinical use.

BET Surface Area Analysis

To quantify the specific surface area, which is crucial for understanding the material's interaction with biological components.

Ex-vivo Studies

To test the material's performance in a more biologically relevant environment, bridging the gap between in-vitro tests and live subjects.

Prothrombin Time (PT) Test

To use an automated blood coagulation analyzer to obtain precise measurements of the clotting cascade initiated by the nanoparticles.
